To start, let’s briefly diagnose the current PLNet architecture:It uses a VGG-style shared backbone (inspired by SuperPoint) that outputs shared feature maps. From there, it splits into two disjoint branches:
1. Keypoint Module: Outputs point heatmaps and descriptors.
2. Line Module: Uses a modified, lightweight U-Net to increase the receptive field, predicting distance fields, angle fields, and junction locations (highly inspired by HAWP - Holistically-Attracted Wireframe Parsing), followed by EPD LOIAlign for line proposal verification.Crucially, the training is done in two disconnected rounds (train points -> freeze backbone -> train lines), and it only detects structural wireframe lines, missing general texture lines.
Here are four distinct architectural improvement directions you can explore for your paper:
1. End-to-End Joint Feature Reasoning (Cross-Attention between Points & Lines)
The Problem: In PLNet, the point and line heads are completely separate after the backbone. Furthermore, the codebase explicitly freezes the backbone and keypoint module during the second round of training the line module. This prevents the network from learning the geometric correlations between keypoints (which are often line junctions) and the lines themselves.The Improvement:
* Architecture: Introduce a lightweight Cross-Attention or Message Passing layer between the keypoint feature maps and the line feature maps before the final prediction heads.
* Why it works: Points and lines are highly co-dependent (e.g., endpoints of a line are keypoints). By allowing the network to cross-reference point heatmaps with line distance fields during inference, you can refine line endpoints directly. You can draw inspiration from the GlueStick paper (which does this for matching) and apply it to detection.
* Training: Design a joint loss function to train the entire network end-to-end, rather than in two frozen stages.
2. Transitioning from "Wireframe" to "General" Lines
The Problem: As noted in the AirSLAM paper, PLNet uses a wireframe parsing method (HAWP-based). This means it is biased towards detecting strict structural lines (e.g., edges of buildings, doors, tables) and explicitly ignores "general" lines (e.g., patterns on the floor, shadows, non-structural contours). In texture-poor or natural environments, discarding general lines weakens the SLAM system.The Improvement:
* Architecture: Modify the Line Module's prediction head. Instead of predicting strict line proposals and junctions via LOIAlign, output a continuous Line Attraction Field (Distance Field + Angle Field) similar to the DeepLSD approach.
* Why it works: You can use these deep gradients to feed into a fast traditional detector (like LSD or ELSED) as a post-processing step. This allows the network to detect all stable lines in the environment, significantly increasing the constraints available for the SLAM backend in unstructured environments.
3. Dynamic Receptive Fields via Deformable Convolutions (DCN)
The Problem: The line module in PLNet utilizes a standard U-Net-like structure to increase the receptive field, which is necessary because lines can stretch across the entire image. However, standard $3 \times 3$ convolutions operate on fixed, square grids, which are poorly suited for extracting elongated, arbitrarily oriented line features.The Improvement:
* Architecture: Replace the standard convolutions in the U-Net bottleneck of the Line Module with Deformable Convolutions (DCNv2 or DCNv3).
* Why it works: Deformable convolutions learn to dynamically adjust their sampling grid based on the input image. For a diagonal line spanning the image, the convolution kernel will stretch along the line's orientation, capturing long-range dependencies much more efficiently than a deep stack of standard convolutions. This could allow you to reduce the depth of the U-Net, increasing FPS while improving line completeness.
4. Multi-Scale Feature Fusion (FPN/BiFPN Integration)
The Problem: According to the codebase, the backbone extracts feature maps at different scales ($1/8$, $1/4$, $1/2$), but the keypoint and line heads primarily operate on a fixed resolution ($1/8$ scale for the SuperPoint head). Lines exist at drastically different scales—some are long structural boundaries, while others are short local details.The Improvement:
* Architecture: Introduce a Bi-directional Feature Pyramid Network (BiFPN) or a lightweight PANet after the VGG-style backbone.
* Why it works: This will fuse high-level semantic information (good for long structural lines and ignoring noise) with low-level spatial details (crucial for precise corner/junction localization). This will make PLNet significantly more robust to the "scale ambiguity" problem in SLAM, especially when the camera moves very close to or very far from obstacles.
Proposed Workflow for Your Paper: "While PLNet successfully unified point and line detection into a single backbone, it treated their final detection as isolated tasks limited to structural wireframes. We introduce Your Network Name, which utilizes Deformable Convolutions to better capture elongated structures and a Cross-Attention feature fusion module to enforce geometric consistency between points and lines end-to-end. Furthermore, we adapt the loss formulation to capture general environmental lines, improving tracking robustness in non-structural environments."
